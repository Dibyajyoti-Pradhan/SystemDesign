Design Netflix

A staff-engineer level system design deep dive into Netflix's architecture, infrastructure, and streaming platform.

1. Overview

Netflix by the Numbers

Netflix is the world's leading streaming entertainment platform, serving over 250 million subscribers across 190+ countries. At peak hours, Netflix accounts for approximately 15% of global internet traffic—more than YouTube, Facebook, and Amazon Prime Video combined.

250M+ Subscribers
15% Global Internet Traffic
15M Concurrent Streams
3 Infrastructure Planes

The scale is staggering: Netflix must reliably deliver video to millions of concurrent viewers across diverse network conditions, device types, and geographic regions—all while maintaining sub-100ms latency and 99.99% availability. Behind this consumer experience lies three distinct infrastructure planes:

  • Control Plane (AWS): Microservices, APIs, user authentication, billing, recommendations, orchestration
  • Data Plane (Open Connect CDN): Video delivery through Netflix's own CDN with ISP-embedded appliances achieving 99%+ cache hit rates
  • Async Plane (Kafka/Spark): Analytics, recommendations retraining, notifications, real-time trending

This design document explores each plane in depth, examining how Netflix solved the hardest problems in distributed systems: scale, latency, reliability, and cost at a global footprint.

2. Clarifying Questions

Scope Definition

Before diving into architecture, clarify scope with the interviewer:

  1. Streaming Quality: Are we designing for video streaming only, or include trailers, previews, and metadata?
  2. Live Events: Support live streaming (sports, concerts), or VOD (video-on-demand) only?
  3. Offline Downloads: Should users be able to download content for offline viewing?
  4. Multiple Profiles: Single user per account, or multiple profiles (kids, family) with separate histories?
  5. DRM & Rights Management: Content protection (Widevine, PlayReady), regional restrictions, concurrent stream limits?
  6. Global Deployment: Design for single region or multi-region active-active architecture?
  7. Monetization Model: Ad-free subscription, ads-supported tier, or both?
Note

For this design, we assume: VOD only, multi-region, no offline downloads, global DRM, multiple profiles, ads-supported tier. These assumptions should be explicit.

3. Functional Requirements

What the System Must Do

Core Flows

  • User Authentication & Profiles: Login, session management, profile selection, account suspension
  • Content Discovery: Search, browse catalog, personalized recommendations (rows), trending content
  • Playback & Streaming: Get playback URL (with CDN edge), adaptive bitrate selection, resume from position, skip intro/credits
  • Viewing History: Track what user watched, when, how long, resume position
  • User Preferences: Subtitles/audio language, video quality (bandwidth cap), parental controls
  • Notifications: New season releases, recommendations, account alerts
  • Billing & DRM: Subscription validation, concurrent stream limits per plan, regional blackouts

Edge Cases

  • Network failover (cellular ↔ WiFi): maintain playback position, resume seamlessly
  • Geographic moves (traveling): switch CDN edge, respect regional content restrictions
  • Concurrent stream limits: enforce plan limits (Basic=1, Standard=2, Premium=4 streams)
  • Stale catalog: user requests content deleted from catalog mid-playback
  • DRM failures: expired license token, device not authorized for HD
  • Cold start problem: new user, zero history, how to personalize recommendations?

5. Capacity Estimation

Back-of-Envelope Math

Concurrent Streams & Bandwidth

  • Peak Concurrent Streams: 15M streams (4pm-11pm US time)
  • Average Bitrate: 5 Mbps (mix of 1080p, 720p, 480p, 360p)
  • Total Bandwidth Required: 15M × 5 Mbps = 75 Tbps peak
  • With OCA Cache Hit Rate 99%: Only 1% = 750 Tbps must hit origin (still massive, S3 + regional caches)

Storage Estimation

  • Catalog Size: ~5,000 titles (estimate)
  • Per-Title Encoding:
    • 1 season = 10 episodes
    • Per episode: 4K (HEVC), 1080p (H.264), 720p (VP9), 480p (H.264), 360p, + audio (English, Spanish, French, Hindi, etc. = ~20 audio tracks)
    • Per episode ≈ 5 GB (averaged across codecs)
    • Per season ≈ 50 GB
    • Average series: 5 seasons = 250 GB per series
    • Movies: 50-100 GB per title (multiple codecs)
  • Total Storage: ~5,000 titles × ~500 GB average = 2.5–3 PB (S3 + replicated to regional CDN caches)

Cache Sizing (EVCache)

  • Viewing History & Metadata (hot): ~100M users active/month, each ~500 KB = 50 TB
  • User Profiles & Preferences: ~50 TB
  • Playback URLs (ephemeral, 24h TTL): ~10 TB
  • Recommendation Rows (refreshed hourly): ~20 TB
  • Total Warm EVCache Size: ~150 TB across fleet
  • Target Hit Rate: 99%+ (achieved through consistent hashing, replication)

Event Streaming

  • Events per Second: 100K–200K (views, searches, clicks, quality metrics)
  • Daily Volume: ~700 billion events
  • Kafka Retention: 7 days (replication factor 3) = ~21 TB raw

6. High-Level Architecture (3 Planes)

The Separation of Concerns

Netflix's architecture elegantly separates concerns into three planes, each optimized for its purpose:

Control Plane (AWS)

The "brains" of Netflix. Hosts all user-facing services, orchestration, and decision-making. Stateless microservices ensure horizontal scaling and fault isolation.

  • API Gateway: Route requests, rate limiting, request validation
  • Microservices: User auth, catalog metadata, recommendations, billing, playback orchestration
  • Persistent stores: MySQL (ACID for billing), Cassandra (high-throughput history), EVCache (hot cache), Elasticsearch (search/indexing)

Data Plane (Open Connect CDN)

The "muscles" of Netflix. Moves bytes at massive scale. Netflix owns its CDN to achieve 99%+ cache hit rates and control edge placement.

  • S3 Origin: Master copy of all encoded videos
  • Regional PoP (Points of Presence): AWS-hosted regional caches
  • ISP-embedded OCA Appliances: Hardware devices placed inside ISP networks for last-mile optimization
  • Proactive nightly push: Popular content pre-cached to edges before peak hours

Async Plane (Kafka/Spark/Flink)

The "learning" engine. Real-time and batch analytics, model retraining, trending detection, notifications.

  • Kafka: Central event hub (700B events/day)
  • Spark: Batch jobs for daily ML model retraining
  • Flink: Real-time streaming for trending content, anomaly detection
  • No user-blocking dependency: failures don't impact playback
Insight

Three-plane separation is critical: Control plane can be in AWS with higher latency tolerance (100ms is fine for API calls). Data plane is owned by Netflix to ensure CDN hit rates and edge control. Async plane is decoupled so analytics don't slow down playback.

7. Open Connect CDN Deep Dive

Netflix's Proprietary Content Delivery Network

Netflix operates Open Connect, its own global CDN with ISP partnerships. Instead of relying on third-party CDNs (Akamai, CloudFront), Netflix maintains direct relationships with ISPs to embed caching appliances in their networks.

OCA Hardware Specs

  • Storage: 100s of TB per appliance (full popular catalog replica)
  • Network: 10–100 Gbps throughput per appliance
  • CPU: Modest compute (mostly acting as transparent HTTP cache)
  • Cost: ~$50K per appliance (amortized over years, saves far more in transit costs)

ISP Peering & Placement Strategy

  • Tier-1 ISPs: Comcast, AT&T, Verizon, Deutsche Telekom, etc. place OCA directly in their infrastructure
  • CDN Economics: Without OCA, Netflix would pay ISPs ~$0.005 per GB for egress (75 Tbps = $375M/month!)
  • OCA Benefit: ~$0.001 per GB or less through peering; breaks even on appliances in months
  • ISP Win: Better streaming QoE for their customers, reduced backbone costs

Cache Strategy: Proactive vs. Reactive

  • Proactive Caching (Nightly Push):
    • Netflix's data science predicts which content will be popular tomorrow (day-of-week patterns, new releases, regional events)
    • Between 11pm–6am: push popular content to regional PoPs, then to ISP appliances
    • Uses BGP-anycast to efficiently route content; ISPs subscribe to feeds
    • Cost: Predictable, off-peak push; reduces peak bandwidth spikes
  • Reactive Caching (Cache Misses):
    • User requests segment not cached locally → fetch from regional PoP → if miss, fetch from S3 origin
    • Origin fetch latency: 200ms (S3 in Virginia → user in Tokyo)
    • Goal: <1% miss rate (99%+ hit rate)

Cache Efficiency Metrics

Metric Netflix Achievement
OCA Cache Hit Rate 99%+
Bandwidth Saved (vs. origin) ~99% (only 1% external traffic)
P99 Latency (OCA hit) 5–15 ms
P99 Latency (origin miss) 100–200 ms
Geographic Coverage 1000+ OCA appliances in 190+ countries
Tip

When asked about CDN, emphasize: Netflix doesn't use third-party CDNs for primary delivery. They built Open Connect for control, cost, and partnership with ISPs. This is a key competitive advantage.

8. Video Encoding Pipeline

Cosmos & Archer: Per-Title Encoding at Scale

Netflix encodes each title to multiple resolutions/codecs, but the pipeline is optimized per title to minimize storage and cost. This is revolutionary: Netflix doesn't use a one-size-fits-all ladder.

Per-Title Encoding Strategy

  • Why Per-Title? Different content types have vastly different encoding characteristics:
    • Animation (cartoon, anime): Low motion, high compressibility → 720p VP9 sufficient
    • Action movies: High motion, fast cuts → needs 1080p H.264 for quality
    • Documentaries: Static camera, slow pans → efficient encoding
  • Codec Ladder: Per device profile:
    • Premium tier (4K TV): 4K HEVC, 1080p H.264, 1080p VP9
    • Standard tier (desktop): 1080p H.264, 720p VP9, 480p H.264
    • Mobile (cellular): 720p H.264, 480p H.264, 360p VP9
  • Cosmos (orchestration): Netflix's internal framework for encoding job scheduling
  • Archer (video analysis): Analyzes each title to determine optimal bitrate ladder

Encoding Cost Optimization

  • Baseline: 5K titles × 10 versions (resolutions/codecs) × encoding time = massive compute
  • Cost: ~$1M+/year on encoding infrastructure (AWS Elemental, internal encoders)
  • Amortization: Spread over production lifetime (film once, stream 10 years)
  • Optimization:
    • Parallel encoding (Cosmos distributes jobs)
    • Hardware acceleration (GPU encoders)
    • Incremental encoding (only re-encode what changes for new seasons)
Note

Per-title encoding is a Netflix innovation. It's not unique anymore (YouTube, AWS Media Services offer it), but Netflix pioneered it to optimize storage/bandwidth under massive scale. This is a key talking point.

9. Adaptive Streaming (DASH)

BOLA Algorithm & Client-Side Bitrate Adaptation

Netflix uses DASH (Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP) with a proprietary bitrate adaptation algorithm called BOLA (Buffer Occupancy-based Bitrate Adaptation).

DASH (Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP)

  • Segmentation: Video split into 2–4 second chunks (fragments)
  • Manifest (MPD): XML describes all available bitrates/resolutions
  • Adaptation: Client requests segments dynamically based on bandwidth
  • Advantage over HLS: More efficient, lower latency, industry standard

BOLA Bitrate Adaptation

  • Core Idea: Maximize quality while avoiding rebuffering by watching buffer occupancy
  • Algorithm:
    • At each segment decision point: measure buffer level, available bandwidth
    • Buffer Utility Function: score each bitrate choice; pick max utility
    • Utility ∝ bitrate quality − penalty for buffer drain
  • Result: Netflix achieves <1/1000 streams with stall rate (rebuffering)

Bitrate Ladder (Example)

Resolution Codec Bitrate Target Device
4K (2160p) HEVC 25 Mbps Smart TV (premium WiFi)
1080p H.264 / VP9 5 Mbps Desktop, tablet
720p H.264 / VP9 2.5 Mbps Smartphone (good WiFi)
480p H.264 1.5 Mbps Smartphone (cellular)
360p H.264 0.5 Mbps Poor connectivity
Tip

Key metric: stall rate < 1/1000. This is Netflix's SLA. Achieving it at 15M concurrent streams requires: proactive caching (OCA), CDN hit rates >99%, smart bitrate adaptation (BOLA), and fallback mechanisms.

10. Microservices Architecture

Zuul, Eureka, Ribbon, Hystrix, and Netflix's OSS Ecosystem

Netflix pioneered the microservices movement and contributed heavily to open-source libraries that the industry uses today.

Core Netflix OSS Components

Component Purpose Why It Matters at Netflix Scale
Zuul (API Gateway) Route requests, filter, throttle, auth Single entry point; centralized rate limiting, request validation, routing rules
Eureka (Service Discovery) Register/discover microservices dynamically Services register on startup; clients query Eureka for healthy instances; handles node failures gracefully
Ribbon (Client-Side LB) Load balance requests across instances Works with Eureka; round-robin, weighted, or custom strategies; detects dead instances
Hystrix (Circuit Breaker) Prevent cascading failures via circuit breaker pattern If downstream service fails, fast-fail and serve fallback; prevent thundering herd
Archaius (Config Management) Dynamic configuration without redeploy Change rate limits, timeouts, feature flags at runtime via property bus
Servo (Metrics) Publish metrics to monitoring system Real-time visibility into service health, latency, errors for alerting

Service Architecture Principles

  • Stateless Services: Any instance can handle any request; state lives in cache/DB
  • Fault Isolation: Service A failure shouldn't impact service B (via circuit breakers, timeouts)
  • Graceful Degradation: If recommendations fail, show fallback (popular titles); playback always works
  • Async Where Possible: Billing, notifications don't block playback request
  • Observability: Every service logs, metrics, traces for debugging

Example Flow: Playback Request

Client → Zuul (API Gateway)
  ├─ Rate limit check (Archaius config)
  ├─ Auth token validation
  └─ Route to Streaming Service via Ribbon (LB)

Streaming Service (via Eureka)
  ├─ Lookup user profile (EVCache hit, ~1ms)
  ├─ Fetch content metadata (Catalog Service, ~20ms, with Hystrix timeout 100ms)
  ├─ Check concurrent stream limit (Billing Service, ~10ms, circuit breaker)
  ├─ Select nearest CDN edge (GeoIP lookup)
  └─ Return playback URL + license token

Response to client: <100ms p95, <200ms p99
Warning

Microservices add complexity. Netflix's mastery of testing, chaos engineering, and observability is what makes it work at scale. Without it, microservices architecture becomes a liability (more failures, hard to debug).

11. Database Architecture

Polyglot Persistence: MySQL, Cassandra, EVCache, Elasticsearch

Netflix doesn't use a single database. Instead, each use case is optimized with the right tool.

Database Use Case Why It's Right
MySQL (RDS) Billing, subscriptions, transactional data ACID guarantees; must not lose money. Netflix uses RDS multi-AZ for failover.
Cassandra Viewing history (billions of records), user events Write-heavy, eventually consistent. Can handle 2.5K+ writes/sec per node. Distributed, no SPOF.
EVCache (memcached) Hot cache: viewing history, playback URLs, recommendations rows Sub-millisecond latency (~1ms); 99%+ hit rate. Distributed hash table with replication.
Elasticsearch Full-text search (titles, descriptions, actors), autocomplete Inverted indexes; faceted search; real-time relevance ranking.
S3 Video files (master copy), metadata, logs Durability, cost-effective for cold storage, integrates with analytics (Spark reading S3).
Druid (OLAP) Real-time analytics dashboards, ad-hoc queries on events Column-oriented; fast aggregations over 700B events/day. Sub-second query latency.

Cassandra Deep Dive

  • Schema: Partition key = user_id; clustering key = timestamp (viewing history ordered by time)
  • Replication: RF=3 (replicate to 3 nodes for durability)
  • Consistency: Writes use LOCAL_QUORUM (2 of 3), then async replicate
  • Scale: 1000+ nodes worldwide; auto-rebalance on node add/removal
  • Cost Optimization: Compress data in Cassandra; archive old history to S3 (cold storage)

EVCache Sizing & Hit Rate

  • Capacity: ~150 TB across fleet (varies by region)
  • Hit Rate: 99%+; keys include:
    • user:123:resume_position (5 KB per user)
    • catalog:title:456 (metadata, artwork)
    • playback:token:xyz (24h TTL)
    • recommendation:user:123 (personalized rows, refreshed hourly)
  • Eviction Policy: LRU (least recently used); when full, evict cold keys
  • Consistency: Eventually consistent; short TTLs (1–24h) avoid stale data
Insight

Polyglot persistence is powerful but requires discipline: Each database adds operational complexity. Netflix invests heavily in tooling, data governance, and team training. Small teams need fewer databases; Netflix's scale justifies the complexity.

12. Recommendation System

3-Stage Candidate Generation, Filtering, and Ranking

Netflix's recommendation system is arguably the most important product: ~80% of watch time comes from recommendations, not search. It's a sophisticated ML pipeline with three stages.

Stage 1: Candidate Generation (10K candidates)

  • Collaborative Filtering (CF):
    • User-user or item-item similarity: if you watched A and B, users who also watched A likely enjoy B
    • SVD / Alternating Least Squares (ALS): matrix factorization to find latent features
    • Output: 10K candidate titles (from 5K catalog)
  • Content-Based Filtering:
    • Genre, cast, director similarity
    • User's history vs. metadata of unwatch titles
  • Trending & Popularity:
    • Real-time trending (Flink on Kafka)
    • What's hot globally, regionally, among your demographic
  • Computational Cost: CF matrices updated daily; billions of multiplications but cached

Stage 2: Filtering (reduce to ~500)

  • Already Watched: Remove titles user finished or abandoned
  • Regional Restrictions: Content not licensed in user's region
  • Parental Controls: Hide age-inappropriate content
  • Concurrent Stream Limits: Don't recommend if user at plan limit
  • Personal Exclusions: User opted out of title

Stage 3: Ranking (select ~50–100)

  • LightGBM Model: Gradient boosting tree model with 150+ features
  • Features include:
    • Predicted CTR (click-through rate): probability user clicks on title
    • Predicted Watch Time: how long user will watch if they click
    • Freshness: new releases score higher
    • Diversity: don't show all action movies (repetitive)
    • Engagement: trending, week's top 10, curated picks
  • Model Training: Retrain daily on Spark with previous day's engagement data
  • A/B Testing: Test ranking changes on 5% of users; measure watch time delta

Example: Homepage Personalization

User Logs In
  ├─ [Stage 1] CF model: fetch 10K candidates from pre-computed matrix
  ├─ [Stage 2] Filter: remove watched, regional blocks, parental controls → 500 titles
  ├─ [Stage 3] Rank: LightGBM scores each; select top 50
  └─ Homepage rows: Top picks, Trending, Sci-Fi recommendations, etc.

Response to client: <500ms p95

Online vs. Offline Evaluation

Metric Measurement Target
CTR (Click-Through Rate) % of users clicking recommended title 2–5% (context-dependent)
Watch Time (primary metric) Hours user watches per month Maximize (A/B test; measure lift)
NDCG (Ranking quality) Offline metric; does ranking order matter? Higher is better; validate before A/B
Diversity % titles from different genres in top-N Balance relevance vs. discovery
Tip

For interviews: Emphasize that recommendations are a ranking problem, not just similarity. Netflix uses a learned model (LightGBM) with business metrics (watch time, not just accuracy). A/B testing is essential to validate improvements.

13. Personalization Signals

How Netflix Learns About You

Netflix collects hundreds of signals to personalize experience. This data fuels the ML models.

Core Signals

  • Viewing History: Every title watched, duration, resume position, completion rate
  • Interactions: Clicks, hovers, searches, sorts, playback quality selected
  • Device Type: TV, desktop, mobile → different content preferences
  • Time-of-Day Patterns: Morning = quick shows; evening = movies (context matters)
  • Engagement Signals: Rate titles (thumbs up/down), add to list, share

Behavioral Signals

  • Scroll Position in Rows: Row #1 vs. Row #5 (left-biased)
  • Dwell Time: How long user hovers on title card (high dwell = interest)
  • Skip Behavior: User immediately skips intro credits (length preference)
  • Context Switching: User pauses movie, switches to another device
  • Binge Behavior: Watch 3 episodes back-to-back (series affinity)

A/B Testing: Artwork & Thumbnails

Netflix A/B tests artwork (title image, thumbnail) because it significantly impacts CTR:

  • Version A: Actor's face (human connection)
  • Version B: Dramatic scene (action emphasis)
  • Version C: Logo + text (clarity)
  • Measurement: Which version has higher CTR?
  • Personalization: Different users see different artwork based on history
  • Example Lift: Personalized artwork can increase CTR by 5–10%

Cold Start Problem

  • New User: No history; how to personalize?
  • Solutions:
    • Onboarding Quiz: ask user to rate 10 sample titles
    • Popular Baseline: show trending titles in user's region/demographics
    • Content-Based: if user rated sci-fi, recommend similar sci-fi
    • Explore-Exploit: mix recommendations with exploration (show diverse content)
Note

Privacy balance: Netflix collects rich data but respects privacy (GDPR, CCPA). Users can manage data, delete history, and opt out of personalization if desired.

14. Data & Analytics Pipeline

Kafka, Spark, Flink, Druid: From Events to Insights

Netflix processes 700 billion events per day to fuel real-time analytics, model training, and operational dashboards.

Event Streaming (Kafka)

  • Events: View start/stop, quality metrics (bitrate, stalls), search, clicks, errors
  • Volume: 100K–200K events/sec = 700B/day
  • Partitioning: By user_id (key) so all user's events go to same partition (order)
  • Retention: 7 days (replicated 3x); after 7 days, archived to S3
  • Latency: Sub-second (published by client immediately after event)

Batch Analytics (Spark)

  • Daily Jobs: Run at 2am UTC (off-peak)
  • Scope: Analyze yesterday's 700B events
  • Tasks:
    • Cohort analysis: engagement trends by region, device, subscription tier
    • ML model retraining: update recommendation models with new data
    • Content performance: which titles have high/low completion rates
    • Quality metrics: stall rates, startup delay, bitrate distributions
  • Infra: 1000s of Spark nodes; Kubernetes for job scheduling

Real-Time Analytics (Flink)

  • Latency: Sub-second to few seconds (vs. 24h for Spark)
  • Use Cases:
    • Trending content: detect spikes in real-time (K-Means clustering)
    • Anomaly detection: unusual stall rate spike → alert ops team
    • Quality monitoring: p95 bitrate drops → trigger investigation
  • Infra: Flink cluster with state backends (RocksDB); at-least-once semantics

OLAP Analytics (Druid)

  • Purpose: Ad-hoc queries by analysts; dashboards
  • Data: Pre-aggregated events (dimensions: user_country, device_type, content_genre, hour)
  • Metrics: Count, sum, avg (completion rate, avg bitrate, stall rate)
  • Query Latency: Sub-second (even over 700B rows) thanks to column-oriented storage
  • Example Query: "Stall rate by ISP, last 7 days, US only, 1080p, rolling hourly"

Data Pipeline Diagram (Conceptual)

Services emit events → Kafka (700B/day, 7-day retention)
                           ↓
                    ┌──────┼──────┬──────────┐
                    ↓      ↓      ↓          ↓
                  Flink  Spark  Stream    Druid
                (real-   (batch) export   (OLAP)
                time)    (daily) (S3)

Flink output → Dashboards, alerts (anomaly detection)
Spark output → ML training, insights, reports
S3 storage   → Long-term archive, ML training data
Druid        → Analytics dashboards, ad-hoc queries
Insight

Event-driven architecture scales: Kafka decouples producers (services) from consumers (Spark, Flink, Druid). If one consumer is slow, others aren't blocked. This is why Netflix uses Kafka for everything—it's the central nervous system.

15. Resilience & Chaos Engineering

Failing Safely at Netflix Scale

Netflix's philosophy: expect failures; build systems that gracefully degrade.

Chaos Engineering Tools

Tool What It Does
Chaos Monkey Randomly terminates EC2 instances in production (during business hours)
Chaos Kong Simulates entire AWS region failure
Latency Monkey Injects artificial delay into service calls (~100ms) to test timeout handling
Security Monkey Finds overly permissive security groups, IAM policies
Conformity Monkey Ensures services follow best practices (use Eureka, implement health checks, etc.)

Design Patterns for Resilience

  • Stateless Services: Any instance can handle any request; no in-memory state. Scale instantly.
  • Multi-Region Active-Active:
    • Multiple AWS regions; traffic load-balanced
    • If one region fails, traffic routes to others automatically
    • Data replicated (eventual consistency for reads; consistency via quorum for writes)
  • Graceful Degradation:
    • Playback always works (core requirement)
    • Recommendations fail? Show fallback catalog
    • Personalization fails? Show trending titles
  • Fast Failover:
    • Health checks every 1s (Eureka); unhealthy instances immediately removed
    • Circuit breakers: if service fails, fast-fail (don't wait for timeout)
    • Bulkheads: isolate thread pools (catalog service timeout doesn't affect billing)
  • Retries with Exponential Backoff:
    • Transient failures (network hiccup): retry 3x with delays (100ms, 200ms, 400ms)
    • Permanent failures (5xx): don't retry; fail fast

Observability (Logging, Metrics, Traces)

  • Logging: Every request logged with correlation ID (trace through entire call chain)
  • Metrics: Count, latency, errors per service (published to Servo/Atlas)
  • Distributed Tracing: Zipkin/Jaeger; visualize request path across services
  • Alerting: Thresholds on latency, error rate, resource usage
Warning

Chaos engineering requires discipline: Testing in production is powerful but risky. Netflix runs chaos experiments during business hours (controlled) and monitors carefully. Smaller companies should start with chaos in staging environments.

16. Failure Modes & Mitigations

8 Critical Failure Scenarios

Failure Impact Mitigation
CDN Region Outage Users in region can't stream (if no fallback) Multi-region CDN; traffic fails over to adjacent PoP. OCA hit rate >99% means only 1% internal traffic.
Cassandra Partition Can't read viewing history; can't resume playback Multi-region replication; quorum consistency (wait for 2/3 replicas). Fallback: serve from cache.
EVCache Flood (all keys evicted) All playback URLs expired; every request hits Streaming Service (slow) Cache warming (pre-populate hot keys on startup). Streaming Service caches responses internally.
Microservice Cascade User Service fails → all requests timeout → Zuul overloaded Circuit breaker (fail fast); bulkheads (separate thread pools); rate limiting at API Gateway.
Database Disk Full Can't write new billing records, viewing history Monitoring for disk usage; auto-scale storage. Archive old history to S3 (cold storage).
Kafka Backlog Accumulation Flink jobs lag (recommendations stale); alerts slow to fire Scale Kafka consumers; use early aggregation. If lag too high, drop old events (bounded state).
DRM License Token Expiry Client can't decrypt video (even with valid subscription) License servers have caches; token refresh endpoint. Client retries if expired.
Encoding Pipeline Backed Up New releases not encoded in time for launch Batch encoding jobs start days before release. Cosmos orchestrates parallel workers. SLA: deliver 1 title in <24h.
Insight

The key to reliability is defense in depth: No single fix prevents all failures. Netflix layers mitigations (multi-region, caching, circuit breakers, monitoring). When one layer fails, others catch it.

17. Trade-offs & Design Decisions

What Netflix Chose and Why

Decision Tradeoff Why This Choice?
Own CDN (Open Connect) Operational complexity vs. third-party CDN convenience Netflix's traffic is 15% of internet. No third-party CDN can optimize for Netflix-scale; OCA saves ~$1B+/year on egress.
Microservices Distributed complexity vs. monolithic simplicity At Netflix scale (thousands of engineers), monolith becomes bottleneck. Microservices enable independent teams, fault isolation.
Cassandra (no strong consistency) Eventual consistency vs. transactional guarantees Viewing history is not mission-critical (lost resume position is annoying, not catastrophic). Cassandra scales to any volume; MySQL would bottleneck.
Segment-based DASH 2–4s chunks vs. finer control 2–4s segments balance latency (can switch bitrate quickly) and complexity (fewer HTTP requests). Longer segments = wasted bandwidth on stalls.
Per-Title Encoding Encoding cost vs. storage/bandwidth savings Encoding is one-time; savings compound over years (video watched billions of times). ROI is massive.
3-Stage Recommendation (CF → Filter → Rank) Latency (3 stages = slower) vs. accuracy (tailored) First two stages are fast (cached, simple logic). Ranking is slow (LightGBM) but only on 500 candidates (not 5K). Pareto principle applies.
Proactive CDN Caching Predictive accuracy vs. storage savings Netflix predicts content popularity accurately (80%+ accuracy for top 20%). Nightly push smooths peak demand; avoids cache misses.
Eventual Consistency (EVCache) 1–2s stale data vs. strong consistency Playback URLs only valid for 24h anyway. Slight staleness acceptable; benefit is <1ms latency vs. 20ms if quorum read.

Opening Statement (First 2 Minutes)

"Netflix is a global streaming platform serving 250M subscribers. The core challenge is reliably delivering video to millions of concurrent viewers across diverse networks while maintaining sub-100ms latency and 99.99% availability.

The system has three distinct planes:
1. Control Plane (AWS): microservices, API, business logic
2. Data Plane (Open Connect CDN): content delivery via Netflix's own CDN
3. Async Plane (Kafka/Spark): analytics, recommendations, notifications

I'll clarify scope, estimate capacity, then dive deep into architecture."

Scenario-Based Follow-ups to Expect

  1. "How do you handle a region failure?"
    → Multi-region active-active; traffic fails over automatically. Data replicated across regions with eventual consistency (CF, Cassandra). Critical data (billing) uses quorum consistency.
  2. "How does the recommendation system handle cold start?"
    → Onboarding quiz (user rates 10 titles). Popular baseline for region/demographics. Explore-exploit balance (80% relevant, 20% exploration).
  3. "Offline downloads: how would you add this?"
    → Client requests download URL; DRM-protected content sent to device. License cached locally. Sync metadata changes when online. Use SQLite on device for manifest.
  4. "Live streaming: how different from VOD?"
    → No advance caching (unpredictable). Ingest stream → segment in real-time → push to PoPs. Lower segment duration (1–2s) for latency. More origin load.
  5. "DRM: how does license delivery work?"
    → Client requests Widevine license from Netflix license server. License specifies resolution rights (4K only on Premium plan), device bindings, expiry. License servers have regional replicas.
  6. "How do you optimize encoding cost?"
    → Per-title analysis (Archer) determines bitrate ladder. Parallel encoding (Cosmos). GPU acceleration. Incremental encoding (only new episodes). Archive old versions.
  7. "Concurrent stream limits enforcement?"
    → Billing Service checks subscription tier. If limit reached, new playback request rejected or previous stream terminated. State stored in Cassandra, cached in EVCache.

Closing Pitch (Last 2 Minutes)

"The Netflix architecture is optimized for three things:

1. Scale: 15M concurrent streams means every component must handle 10K+ requests/sec. Stateless services, caching (EVCache), and distributed databases (Cassandra) enable this.

2. Latency: Sub-100ms API and <5ms CDN delivery require Open Connect (owned CDN), proactive caching, and geographic edge placement. No third-party CDN can do this at Netflix scale.

3. Reliability: Multi-region active-active, circuit breakers, chaos engineering, and graceful degradation ensure 99.99% availability. When something fails, playback continues; other features degrade.

The biggest lesson: Netflix invests heavily in operational excellence (observability, tooling, chaos engineering). Architecture alone doesn't scale; culture + tooling + discipline do."

Conclusion

Netflix System Design Summary

Netflix's architecture is a masterclass in distributed systems at scale. Key takeaways:

  • 3-Plane Separation: Control (AWS), Data (Open Connect CDN), Async (Kafka/Spark) enables independent optimization
  • Ownership of Infrastructure: Netflix built Open Connect CDN to achieve 99%+ cache hit rate and control economics
  • Per-Title Encoding: Tailored bitrate ladders per content type; encodes once, streams billions of times
  • Microservices + Chaos Engineering: Expect failures; build systems that gracefully degrade
  • Polyglot Persistence: Right tool for right use case (MySQL for billing, Cassandra for history, EVCache for hot cache)
  • Sophisticated ML: 3-stage recommendations (CF → filter → rank) power 80% of watch time
  • Event-Driven Analytics: Kafka ingests 700B events/day; Spark/Flink/Druid extract insights

When you design systems at Netflix scale, you're solving problems that very few companies face. That's what makes this interview question so valuable: it exposes how you think about distributed systems, trade-offs, and user-centric design.

Insight

Final thought: Netflix's secret sauce isn't the technology (Kafka, Cassandra, etc. are all open-source). It's the discipline: obsessive focus on quality metrics (watch time, stall rate), culture of chaos engineering, and willingness to invest in custom infrastructure. When you interview, focus on these principles rather than just listing technologies.
